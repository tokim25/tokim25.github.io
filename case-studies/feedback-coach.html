Case Study · Take-home Assignment · Stripe

Nobody needs the whole framework. They need help in the next five minutes.

Feedback Coach is a Slack-based coach built on Stripe's own SBIN framework, made as a take-home assignment for a Learning Architect role. Not internal Stripe work, and the outcome of that application isn't part of this write-up.

Try it yourself: click through a real coaching session below.

The Problem

The framework was fine. The moment it was needed wasn't.

Most people never finish reading Stripe's 2,000-word SBIN framework page. The gap wasn't the content, it was personalized help applying it right when it's hardest to think straight: right before a real feedback conversation, or right after one that didn't go the way you'd hoped.

Design

Two modes, nothing heavier. Prepare structures what you want to say before a real conversation. Debrief breaks down how one actually went after the fact. No upfront self-assessment gate, people land in the mode they need and start talking.

Voice

I synthesized a register from Stripe's own Operating Principles and marketing voice: direct and warm. A vulnerable moment needs candor, not corporate distance.

Coaching logic

The coach is grounded in SBIN and trained on Stripe's own voice, so its advice sounds like Stripe rather than something generic. People share their actual situations, not hypotheticals, and get advice specific to what they wrote. It lives in Slack, where people already talk to their colleagues.

Key Design Decisions

The calls that shaped the build.

Guardrail

Any attempt to draft real review content gets redirected to Stripe's actual approved tool. This coach is for everyday practice, not formal review documentation, and it says so upfront.

Coach, don't force

One nudge per SBIN component, then move on. Pushback gets logged as a growth area instead of blocking the conversation.

A rubric to score against

Scoring a live draft takes a structured judgment call, not just conceptual guidance, so I built a Weak / Strong / Stronger rubric myself. It's labeled as my own construction, pending real validation, not Stripe's official stance.

Character clarity

Colored tags and a "stepping out" line mark the difference between the coach role-playing a conversation and the coach actually coaching, so it's never ambiguous which mode you're in.

Working With Claude

Delegation

Claude handled the research, drafting, and the Figma build. The learning outcomes, scope calls, and anything touching a real person's rubric or privacy stayed with me.

Description

I gave Claude real constraints: the SBIN framework, a hard line between everyday coaching and formal review, and I asked for options and reasoning before anything got built.

Discernment

I caught a writing-style audit that hadn't actually run until I checked.

Diligence

The scoring rubric and practice scenarios are labeled as my own construction, pending real validation. The privacy language is explicit placeholder text, pending stakeholder review.

Measurement Plan

Three signals, not one score.

Usage

Slack analytics and click-throughs to the guide, tracked monthly

Effectiveness

SBIN rubric score trend on each person's weakest component, monthly

Behavior change

Fewer vague or character-judgment patterns in real feedback, aggregated cohorts, quarterly
